LMP2 24H Le Mans Preparation

End-to-end race engineering workflow for a 5-driver amateur endurance team. Setup development through three versions in response to a mid-preparation BOP change, structured driver feedback and coaching, multi-timezone stint strategy, driver coaching in Garage 61, and in-depth setup analysis in Cosworth Pi Toolbox.

Context

The Challenge

The 24 Hours of Le Mans on iRacing is a full-duration endurance event requiring the same core disciplines as real-world endurance racing: setup development, driver management, fuel and stint strategy, and real-time decision-making. For a 5-driver amateur team with varying skill levels and schedules across three time zones, delivering a competitive and safe car requires a structured engineering approach rather than informal trial-and-error.

A key constraint arrived mid-preparation when iRacing released an unexpected Balance of Performance (BOP) update affecting the LMP2 class. The mandated weight increase required a front ride height increase, directly degrading the car's aerodynamic balance and introducing high-speed understeer that needed to be resolved within the limits of a fixed Le Mans aero package.

Engineering Constraint
The Le Mans aero package is fixed — no wing angle adjustment available. All balance corrections must come from mechanical setup: geometry, springs, dampers, and differential. This significantly narrows the available tools compared to a standard circuit configuration.
Setup Development

Three-Version Iteration

Setup development followed a structured versioning approach, with each version documented against the full parameter set and cross-referenced with driver feedback data. Changes between versions were made deliberately with specific objectives, not through random adjustment.

Version 1 — Baseline
Initial Setup Development
Established baseline setup from scratch for the Le Mans aero package. Medium heave spring, ARB front P2 / rear P3, symmetric torsion bars at 3.2 turns, diff at 45/70 coast with 110 Nm preload. Starting pressures at 152 kPa across all four corners. Provided a neutral, driveable baseline to benchmark team feedback against.
Version 1.5 — Drivability Focus
Entry & Exit Refinement
Addressed team feedback requesting improved corner entry response and braking consistency. Front ARB moved to P3 (stiffer), rear ARB to P2 (softer), third spring gap reduced from 19.6 to 18.8 mm for tighter pitch control. Differential coast opened from 70 to 55 and preload reduced to 100 Nm to improve off-throttle turn-in. Brake bias pushed forward to 57.2% and front master cylinder increased to 22.2 mm in support of fuel-saving deep-braking strategy. Mid-corner balance scores improved significantly in driver feedback.
Version 2 — BOP Response
Balance Recovery Post-BOP
Mandatory BOP weight increase required front ride height to increase from 24.7 mm to 25 mm, reducing rake and degrading front downforce relative to the rear. With the Le Mans aero package fixing wing angles, balance recovery was pursued through front geometry — front torsion bar turns reduced to 3.15, rear toe-in increased to 1.7 mm to improve rear stability. Rear ride height evaluation ongoing: a conservative increase toward 54 mm would partially restore rake and front aero contribution without significant straight-line drag penalty.
Driver Development

Structured Feedback Protocol

A structured driver feedback form was developed and deployed across the team to replace informal verbal feedback. Each session is rated across seven metrics: driver confidence, entry stability, mid-corner balance, exit traction, kerb confidence, fatigue over stint, and overall pace — all correlated against track temperature and session conditions.

This approach separates driver feel from setup performance by identifying which phase of the corner is causing issues rather than collecting vague "the car feels wrong" feedback. It also allows comparison across five drivers with different experience levels and driving styles, making setup regressions and improvements measurable.

Team Management

Multi-Driver Setup Philosophy

Managing a setup for five drivers with different skill levels and preferences requires a fundamentally different approach than single-driver optimization. The team spans CST, EST, and PST time zones with a range of experience from national-level sim racing to novice endurance driving.

The key constraint: two of the fastest drivers prefer oversteer, while the safest and most consistent drivers either prefer mild understeer or need a predictable, non-aggressive car to perform at their best. The BOP-induced understeer, while problematic for outright pace, actually provides a more stable platform for the lower-confidence drivers — a genuine tradeoff that was consciously accepted rather than simply fixed.

Driver coaching was conducted through live sessions — reviewing telemetry while co-driving — to identify specific braking and weight transfer issues on a per-corner basis at Dunlop and the Mulsanne chicanes, then providing targeted feedback rather than generic advice.

Race Strategy

24-Hour Stint Planning

The full event was mapped as 32 stints of 0:45 each, grouped into 16 back-to-back double-stint pushes so every driver runs ~90 minutes per relief — minimising tyre warm-up cycles and driver swaps versus single stints. Real EST race clock, in-sim time-of-day, and per-driver availability were tracked in parallel across three US time zones.

Rotation order is built around the time-zone spread: the PST driver is slotted into late-EST windows that fall in his evening, while the EST and CST drivers absorb the graveyard hours. The most consistent drivers take the highest incident-risk windows — clean laps matter more than peak pace when the 100-incident limit is the real constraint.

Fuel & Energy

Lift-and-Coast Fuel Saving

Baseline burn on a full-push lap measured 6.96 L/lap. Lifting off the throttle early into the three highest-speed braking zones — both Mulsanne chicanes and the approach to Indianapolis — coasts the car onto the brakes and brings consumption down to a 6.60 L/lap target: a 0.36 L, ~5.2% saving per lap with negligible laptime cost.

These three points were chosen deliberately. They carry the highest entry speeds on the lap, so closing the throttle 50–100 m early sheds the most kinetic energy for the least time lost — energy that would otherwise be scrubbed off by the brakes anyway. The consistent brake markers established in v1.5 made the lift point repeatable lap-after-lap, so the saving held up through the overnight hours rather than drifting with driver fatigue.

Telemetry Workflow

Driver Profiling via Telemetry

Two tools split the workload. Garage 61 is the coaching platform — every driver's laps sync to a shared library, so sessions are spent overlaying a driver against the team benchmark and working specific corners. For in-depth setup work the data moves into Cosworth Pi Toolbox — the same software used by WEC and IndyCar teams — for braking-trace, channel, and throttle-application analysis that informs setup direction.

Overlaying each driver's speed trace against the team's benchmark lap turns subjective "the car feels loose" feedback into a quantified, per-corner signature. The Porsche Curves overlay below isolates exactly where pace is found or lost — brake point, minimum apex speed, and how early the driver returns to full throttle — which feeds directly into coaching priorities and the multi-driver setup compromise.
