Formula SAE Pushrod Assembly Design

Structural design and analysis of the front and rear pushrod suspension assembly — rocker arms, pushrods, and damper packaging — for Lumberjack Racing's 2025 Formula SAE car (NAU, Team 072). The challenge: raise the structural margin and refine the motion ratio while constraining every part to what the team's waterjet cutter could produce. A manufacturing-constrained structural problem with competing objectives across strength, weight, and buildability.

Context

The Problem

Formula SAE suspension systems operate under complex, multi-directional load inputs. The pushrod assembly — pushrod, rocker arm, and damper mount — sits at the interface between the upright and the spring/damper unit, transmitting and transforming wheel loads into controlled damper inputs. Getting the geometry right has cascading effects: the motion ratio, damper stroke envelope, and wheel rate all depend on rocker arm geometry.

The inherited car placed 97th overall the prior year, hampered by limited suspension adjustability and components that bottomed out under load. The previous pushrod setup ran a motion ratio of 1 on a 200 lb/in spring and frequently bottomed from minor inputs. The mandate was to fix the geometry and the structural margin together — within one hard constraint: every component had to be manufacturable on the team's waterjet cutter, with only mill and lathe available for finishing.

The Core Tension
Waterjet cutting is a 2D process. The loads on a rocker arm are 3D — bending, torsion, and shear acting simultaneously. Designing a component that handles 3D loads using a 2D manufacturing process requires deliberate geometry decisions that a standard machined part wouldn't need.
Design Process

Engineering Decisions

Damper selection. Top teams run Öhlins TTX25 MkII, Penske 8760, and Koni 2812 dampers — excellent but expensive. After benchmarking, I selected the Fox DHx2 as the best balance of cost and adjustability for our program, then designed the rocker geometry and damper packaging around its stroke envelope.

Spring rate from first principles. A worst-case roll analysis (combined roll, bump, and braking) produced a vertical tire reaction approaching 4000 N. Running that through the pushrod geometry in MATLAB at a 1.4 motion ratio gave ~3100 N at the spring across 1.8 in of travel — sizing a 500 lb/in spring rate. That's a deliberate step up from last year's 200 lb/in (motion ratio 1), which is exactly why the old car bottomed out.

Manufacturing-driven geometry. With limited CNC access, the rocker was designed to be mostly waterjet-cut with only mill/lathe finishing for bores and bearing seats. Strength was added not through a 3D-machined section but through plate-thickness increases in the highest-stress regions and generous fillets at the pivot bore — features a 2D waterjet handles cleanly with no tool change.

Structural Analysis

FEA & Two Rocker Iterations

The rocker arm went through two main iterations. Version 1 pushed weight saving aggressively to reveal where the part actually needed material; the ANSYS results then drove a more conservative Version 2. Modelling the damper assembly in SolidWorks showed I needed clearance for the damper at 40° of full up-travel, which set the dimension changes below — all while holding a 1.5 safety factor.

DriverBearing fit + damper clearance

The rear rocker took the front's weight-optimisation lessons further, using a truss-like profile at slightly greater thickness, and converged after a single iteration. Both rockers are aluminium with grade-8 steel hardware, water-jetted then finished on the mill and lathe.

For the pushrod I ran Euler buckling on the aluminium tube and verified it in ANSYS through eight ANSYS / SolidWorks iterations. The final pushrod cleared a minimum safety factor of 1.367 under the calculated load.

Euler Buckling — Pushrod Sizing
Pcr = π²EI / (KL)²  —  with E = 10×10⁶ psi (aluminium), I = π(0.25 in)⁴⁄4 = 0.00307 in⁴, K = 1.0, L = 22.1 in  →  Pcr = 620.4 lbf critical load.

The rocker was then validated under the calculated worst-case loads. Stress concentrates along the pushrod load path and at the pivot bore; peak von Mises reached 71.2 MPa with maximum deflection of just 13.6 µm. The safety-factor plot reads a 1.21 minimum only at the hardware-simulation filler bodies — the rocker structure itself holds the 1.5 target.

Design Output

SolidWorks Models

The full assembly — pushrods, front and rear rockers, bearing inserts, and damper mounts — was modelled in SolidWorks, with the rocker geometry built around the Fox DHx2 stroke envelope. Parts were drawn for waterjet DXF export: flat profiles, accessible bores, and no geometry needing secondary machining beyond bearing press-fits.

A full-car model was also built in Blender to support early simulator development, letting the team evaluate packaging and build driver familiarity with the car before fabrication was complete.

Outcome

Results & Takeaways

Both rockers and pushrods cleared their structural targets in FEA (rocker SF 1.5, pushrod SF 1.367) while staying fully waterjet-manufacturable. On-car, the spring-rate test confirmed the design intent: ~0.75 in of uptravel under static vehicle weight, with rate increasing as load grew — a progressive stiffness curve that matched the calculated 500 lb/in target and directly addressed last year's bottoming-out problem.

The core lesson: manufacturing constraints are design inputs, not obstacles. The waterjet limit forced explicit geometry decisions a machined part wouldn't need — producing an assembly that is simpler to build, easier to inspect, and more repeatable than a complex machined component would have been.
